üí° Why Study Statistics for Economics?

The lecture highlights that while economic forecasting is more complicated than astronomy , it is arguably more important for society.

    In Business: A manager might use forecasting to decide how many machines to install in a factory. Getting this right benefits consumers and employees; getting it wrong can put the firm in trouble.

In Government: An economist working for the UK government might use data (like birthrates) to plan how many nurses the NHS needs and where in the country they will be needed most.

This module focuses on analyzing data, not on specific economic theories (like Keynesian or Neoclassical economics).

üßÆ Core Topic: Descriptive Statistics

This is the main academic subject of Lecture 1. Descriptive statistics are used to summarize and make sense of data , such as the table of UK GDP growth rates.

A 2023 paper by Meade & Driver states that forecasts can be summarized by "measures of location" and "measures of dispersion". This lecture introduces both.

1. Measures of Location (Central Tendency)

This is a single, "typical" number that represents a whole column of data. They are often just called an "average".

    Mean:

        What it is: The most common type of average.

        How to Calculate: Add up all the values and divide by the number of values.

Excel: =AVERAGE(...)

Median:

    What it is: The middle value in a sorted dataset.

    How to Calculate:

        Sort the data (e.g., highest to lowest).

Repeatedly remove the highest and lowest values.

Continue until only one or two values are left. If two values remain, the median is the mean of those two.

Excel: =MEDIAN(...)

Mode:

    What it is: The value that occurs most often.

Note: A dataset might not have a single mode (e.g., if several values appear twice, but no value appears three times).

Excel: =MODE(...)

Which "Average" to Use? Different measures have different advantages:

    Use Mean: When you need a measure that is related to other statistical concepts like variance.

Use Median: When your data has a few extreme values (outliers) or potential typos. The median is more "resilient" to these extremes.

Use Mode: Best for non-continuous data. For example, a shoe shop might use the mode to decide which shoe size to put in the window display.

2. Measures of Dispersion (Variability)

This measures how much variation or "spread" exists in a set of numbers.

    Variance:

        Concept: A common way to measure dispersion. Simply subtracting the mean from each value doesn't work, as the positive and negative differences cancel out, and the total sum is always zero.

How to Calculate:

    First, calculate the mean of your data (e.g., 1.7).

For each value (X) in your data, find its difference from the mean: (X‚Äìmean).

Square each of those differences: (X‚Äìmean)2. This step is key as it makes all the resulting numbers positive.

Sum (add up) all of these squared differences: ‚àë(X‚Äìmean)2.

Finally, divide this total sum by the number of rows (n): Variance=n‚àë(X‚Äìmean)2‚Äã.

    Example from slide: The sum of squared differences was 264.03, and there were 24 rows. So, 264.03/24=11 (approximately).

Standard Deviation:

    Concept: This is another, very common way to measure variation. It is directly related to variance.

Key Fact: If the standard deviation is zero, it means all the numbers in your list are identical.

How to Calculate: It is simply the square root of the variance.

    Example from slide: Variance=11.001. The standard deviation is 11.001‚Äã, which is approximately 3.317 .
