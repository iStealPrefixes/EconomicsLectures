A key theme of this lecture is understanding the difference between a population and a sample.

    Population: This is the entire group you are interested in. It doesn't always mean people. For example, a population could be "all firms in the UK".

Sample: This is a subset drawn from that population.

We use probability to understand how a random sample relates to the population it came from. This process is the basis for statistical inferenceâ€”using data from a sample to draw conclusions about the whole population.

ðŸŽ² What is Probability?

Probability is essential for economists because it allows us to understand and interpret sample evidence.

1. Key Definitions

    Experiment: An activity with a range of possible outcomes (e.g., tossing a coin).

Trial: A single performance of an experiment (e.g., one coin toss).

Sample Space: The complete list of all possible outcomes.

    Coin toss example: {Heads, Tails}.

Die roll example: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.

Mutually Exclusive Outcomes: Outcomes that cannot happen at the same time in a single trial. You can't get both a head and a tail on one toss.

Event: A specific combination of outcomes. For example, the event "rolling an even number" corresponds to the outcomes {2, 4, 6}.

2. Views on Probability

    Frequentist View: The proportion of times an event happens if you repeat the trial an infinite number of times.

Subjective View: Someone's personal degree of belief that an outcome will occur.

ðŸ”„ Rules for Compound Events

Compound events involve more than one outcome , using rules for "Or," "And," and "Given That".

1. The "Or" Rule (Addition Rule)

This rule is for finding the probability of event A or event B happening.

    General Formula (Always Works):

    Pr(A or B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B) â€“ Pr(A and B) 

    We must subtract Pr(A and B) if the events are not mutually exclusive (i.e., they can happen at the same time).

Example: Pr(King or Heart) = Pr(King) + Pr(Heart) â€“ Pr(King of Hearts) = 4/52 + 13/52 â€“ 1/52 = 16/52. This is because the 'King of Hearts' gets counted twice (once as a King, once as a Heart).

Simplified Formula (Mutually Exclusive Events ONLY):

    If A and B cannot happen together (they are mutually exclusive ), then Pr(A and B) = 0.

The formula simplifies to: Pr(A or B) = Pr(A) + Pr(B).

Example: A card cannot be both an Ace and a King. Pr(Ace or King) = Pr(Ace) + Pr(King) = 4/52 + 4/52 = 8/52.

2. The "And" Rule (Multiplication Rule)

This rule is for finding the probability of event A and event B happening.

    General Formula (using Conditional Probability):

    Pr(A and B) = Pr(A) Ã— Pr(B|A) 

    Pr(B|A) is the conditional probability: "the probability of B happening, given that A has already happened".

Example: What is the probability of drawing a second Ace, given that the first card was an Ace?. After taking one Ace, there are 3 Aces left in a deck of 51 cards. So, Pr(Second Ace | First Ace) = 3/51.

Simplified Formula (Independent Events ONLY):

    Two events are independent if one event happening has no effect on the probability of the other.

If A and B are independent, then Pr(B|A) is the same as Pr(B).

The formula simplifies to: Pr(A and B) = Pr(A) Ã— Pr(B).

Example: Rolling two dice. The outcome of the first die is independent of the second. Pr(Six and Six) = Pr(Six) Ã— Pr(Six) = (1/6) Ã— (1/6) = 1/36.
